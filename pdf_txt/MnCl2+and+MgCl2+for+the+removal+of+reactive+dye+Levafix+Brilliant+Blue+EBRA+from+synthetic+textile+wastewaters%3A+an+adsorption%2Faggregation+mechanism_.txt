Abstract: Waste valorization is attracting not only the scientiÔ¨Åc world but also the world. Acetyl-
cellulose wastes from cigarette Ô¨Ålter residuals are signiÔ¨Åed as the largest global impact of solid
waste. Acetylcellulose recycling for desired products is a promising way for environmental man-
agement. In this regard, the current investigation is dealing with the immersion of residual Ô¨Ålters
sequentially into aqueous solutions of alkali and ethanol before water washing, which converts them
into a superhydrophobic acetylcellulose adsorbent material. The morphology and characteristics
of the acetylcellulose Ô¨Åber were characterized using a Scanning Electron Microscope (SEM) and
Fourier-Transform infrared spectroscopy (FTIR). The adsorption tendency was checked for LevaÔ¨Åx
Blue dye compromised in an aqueous stream as a model textile polluted efÔ¨Çuent. The experimental
results exposed that the acetylcellulose Ô¨Åber displayed a sensible textile dye elimination from the
dying stream. Langmuir isotherm is well Ô¨Åtting the adsorption matrix and the reaction follows the
1st-order kinetic model. The so-obtained acetylcellulose Ô¨Åber showed tremendous efÔ¨Åciency for
dye removal from aqueous efÔ¨Çuent. The attained maximum monolayer adsorption capacity was
recorded as 4.8 mg/g at pH 3.0 and an adsorbent dose of 1 g/L through the isotherm time of 2 h.
Also, temperature elevation could increase the adsorption capacity to 5.7 mg/g. Due to this excellent
afÔ¨Ånity to adsorb dye at an economic wise rate is shown as a promising candidate for textile dye
elimination form aqueous efÔ¨Çuent.

1. Introduction
Since the twentieth century, there has been an ever-increasing alarm for the existence
of solid waste that causes extreme toxicity to the environment [ 1]. To a large extent, the
presence of such waste in the environment is causing ecological damage [ 2]. This necessi-
tates appropriate adequate treatment and management before eliminating their disposal
into the ecosystem. Hence, converting such waste-based materials for new ecologically
benign substances is attaining the scientiÔ¨Åc community. In this concept there has been some
very recent but limited progress in the search for greener materials from solid waste [2,3].
Worldwide, the most littered solid waste is signiÔ¨Åed as a cigarette Ô¨Ålter [ 4‚Äì6]. Trillions
of cigarette Ô¨Ålters are signiÔ¨Åed as a hazard solid waste and is deposited per annum. Instead,
97% of such Ô¨Ålters were compromised as a modiÔ¨Åed natural polymer that is so-called acetyl-
cellulose Ô¨Åber (ACF) [ 4]. Although this acetylcellulose waste is categorized as undervalued
material, the scientists‚Äô and researchers‚Äô interest is seeking sustainable elucidations for
tackling cigarette Ô¨Ålter pollution concerns [ 5]. Till now, generally, solid waste pollution

management includes landÔ¨Ålling incineration, recycling, and degradation [ 7]. Numerous
research works have been conducted to convert cigarette Ô¨Ålter waste into reusable material
for various applications to attain the circular economy concept [ 8]. For instance, it has pre-
viously been applied in the environmental engineering Ô¨Åeld, infrastructures and building
materials, metallurgical engineering, and energy storage devices. However, according to
the research cited [ 9] minimal application of cigarette Ô¨Ålters in environmental engineering,
compared to wide applications in buildings and structure Ô¨Åelds.
An encouraging technique to control the environmental impacts of cigarette Ô¨Ålters is
to convert them into desired products in a circular economy concept. Acetylcellulose [ 10]
which is an economically attractive and usable adsorbent material is the main constituent
of cigarette Ô¨Ålters. Furthermore, acetylcellulose Ô¨Åber (ACF) was previously reported in
the literature [ 11‚Äì15] as an excellent substance with great potential for adsorption capacity.
That is a superior substance for the Ô¨Åeld of separation.
The contamination of dyes has developed into a signiÔ¨Åcant environmental issue
with the development of the textile industry. Due to their severe toxic effectiveness, and
non-biodegradable and bioaccumulated characteristics, dyes in aqueous media pollution
pose a serious threat to the environmental biosphere and public health [ 16]. Due to the
consumption of massive amounts of water in textile dying processes, an ultimate amount
of wastewater is generated. Such wastewater mainly contains unconsumed dyes and
chemical activities that represent a great environmental concern worldwide [ 17,18]. The
result is a direct threat to the aquatic system when these efÔ¨Çuents are not treated before
their Ô¨Ånal disposal [ 6,19‚Äì21]. However, the textile discharge is composed of toxic and
stable substances that are difÔ¨Åcult to remove. Textile industry discharges pose high loads of
various dyes exhibiting vivid colors and posing signiÔ¨Åcant challenges for degradation using
conventional methods. These characteristics result in reduced water transparency, leading
to the obstruction of solar radiation penetration into the ecosystem which is essential for
photosynthetic processes. The result is a massive disturbance to the aquatic creatures [6].
Therefore, for the great signiÔ¨Åcance of such aqueous waste, it must undergo treat-
ment and management. EfÔ¨Çuent discharged from textile industries is eliminated based
on various techniques including physicochemical treatments such as adsorption [ 20], co-
agulation, sedimentation, and Ô¨Åltration. Also, biological treatments based on activated
sludge are applied [ 21]. Such techniques unfortunately are unfavorable since they generate
a large amount of sludge by-product and are not efÔ¨Åcient at destroying or eliminating dye
molecules from aqueous efÔ¨Çuent [ 6]. Thus, the appropriate adsorption system offers a
Ô¨Çexible design for efÔ¨Åcient and economic operation concerning high superiority to reclaim
water [ 17,20]. However, natural and waste materials have attracted interest as alternatives
to synthetic adsorbents due to economic regard and chemical consumption [6,21‚Äì24]. It is
noteworthy to mention that acetylcellulose is signiÔ¨Åed as an inert polymer, that is benign
and odor-free. Hence, the recycling of smuggled cigarette Ô¨Ålters for pollutant elimination is
extremely attractive [ 25‚Äì28]. Notably, acetylcellulose could be derived from fruit branches
and other naturally occurring substances [ 29‚Äì34]. However, using acetylcellulose from
cigarette Ô¨Ålter waste sources is signiÔ¨Åcant since its presence in such waste material causes
massive harm to the environment. Acetylcellulose is a photodegradable polymer that
is not easily biodegradable therefore, its occurrence may persist in the environment for
years [32‚Äì36].
The uptake of contaminants from aqueous efÔ¨Çuents using waste valorized materials is
vital not just for toxicity exclusion, but also for valued waste recovery. The application of
the adsorption technique for the determination of different analyses in different matrices
is a valuable alternative due to the availability of a large range of valuable adsorbents.
Good adsorbent properties that involve high surface area, high removal efÔ¨Åciency, and
reusability support them to be good candidates for environmental remediation [ 19,21]. In
this sense, this current investigation aims to prepare an economic adsorbent from the waste
cigarette Ô¨Ålters for the adsorption of reactive dye in an aqueous medium. The capacity of
the material for LevaÔ¨Åx blue adsorption and the effects of different parameters, such as theProcesses 2023 ,11, 2701 3 of 17
isotherm time, the aqueous media pH, temperature, adsorbent dose, and dye loading are
investigated. Also, the adsorption kinetics and isotherm models are assessed to evaluate
the adsorption system.
2. Experimental Investigation
2.1. Acetylcellulose Fibers Collection and Preparation
Acetylcellulose Ô¨Åbers derived from cigarette Ô¨Ålters are used as an adsorbent material.
Initially, the cigarette Ô¨Ålters are collected from a Coffee shop station near the University
of MenouÔ¨Åa, Egypt before subjection for cleaning and treatment. Initially, the smoked
Ô¨Ålters are de-warped from the covered paper and subjected to successive washing with
distilled water. Then it is ultrasonically cleaned in ethanol alcohol to remove any remaining
impurities. Subsequently, the cleaned Ô¨Åber is removed from the solution by Ô¨Åltration.
Afterward, the samples were subjected to oven drying at 105C to eliminate any moisture
content before their utilization. Then, ACF samples were stored in a dry place at room
temperature for further use.
2.2. Wastewater
LevaÔ¨Åx Blue CA is signiÔ¨Åed as a kind of reactive dye and was delivered by DyStar
Ltd., (Raunheim, Germany) where the aqueous efÔ¨Çuent containing a synthetic textile dye
was employed as a representative model. LevaÔ¨Åx Blue dye is a dark blue powder, which is
categorized as a bifunctional and combined anchor. The dye powder was applied for use
with no further puriÔ¨Åcation or treatment. The aqueous solution is arranged by preparing a
stock solution of 1000 mg/L of the dye, which was then diluted as needed to the required
concentrations.
2.3. Characterisation Study
The sludge samples that were prepared underwent examination and imaging using a
Field-emission scanning electron microscope (FE-SEM, Quanta FEG 250). Fourier Trans-
form Infrared (FTIR) spectra analysis of acetylcellulose (performed using Jasco, FT/IR-4100,
type A) was conducted to determine the speciÔ¨Åc functional groups involved in the adsorp-
tion test. Additionally, the particle size distribution of the acetylcellulose material was
determined using the IMAGEJ 1.48 V program.
2.4. Methodology and Analytical Determination
100-mL of LevaÔ¨Åx Blue CA dye-containing wastewater was poured into a glass con-
tainer to investigate the dye loading on its removal efÔ¨Åciency. The initial solution of the
aqueous environment‚Äôs pH was adjusted, when needed, to the desired values by using di-
luted H 2SO4(1:9) and/or 1.0 M NaOH solutions. The desired pH values were achieved by
utilizing a digital pH meter (Model AD1030, Adwa instrument, Szeged, Hungary) to make
necessary adjustments. Subsequently, ACF-material was added to the wastewater, and the
reaction was conducted for dye reduction. Subsequently, after selected time intervals, sam-
ples were withdrawn periodically and subjected to spectrophotometric analysis after the
samples were Ô¨Åltered using a micro-Ô¨Ålter. Spectrophotometric performances were applied
using Model Unico UV-2100, USA at the maximum absorbance peak of a wavelength of
610 nm which was monitored and applied to evaluate the amount of residual LevaÔ¨Åx Blue
CA dye remaining in the aqueous efÔ¨Çuent after adsorption.

4. Conclusions
Acetlycellulose was extracted and prepared from augmented cigarette Ô¨Ålter waste in
this study. The Ô¨Åber was extracted by treatment and washing to be available as adsorbent
material. The material is examined and analyzed using SEM and FTIR before and after
cleaning. The attained acetlycellulose Ô¨Åber was estimated to be an efÔ¨Åcient adsorbent,
which revealed excellent dye removal efÔ¨Åcacy. The adsorption afÔ¨Ånity reached 4.8 mg/g at
the optimized conditions of pH 6.0 and adsorbent dose 1 g through the isotherm time of 2 h.
Isotherm modeling and kinetic data demonstrated the reaction follows 1st-order kinetics
and the reaction follows Langmuir isotherm. Not only does such an investigation delivers
a potential method for dye elimination, but also speciÔ¨Åes a way to realize the resource
utilization of waste acetlycellulose by-product from smoking residuals to satisfy the goal of
the industrial ecology approach. Therefore, a complimentary effort is still essential in the
future study to apply such technique in real polluted efÔ¨Çuents.
