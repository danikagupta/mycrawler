  Abstract  1 
Amyloid protein aggregates are not only associated with neurodegenerative diseases and may also 2 
occur as unwanted by -product s in protein -based therapeutics. Surfactants are often employed to 3 
stabilize protein formulations and reduce the risk of aggregation. However, surfactants alter protein - 4 
protein interactions and may thus modulate the physico chemical characteristics of any aggregates 5 
formed.  Human insulin aggregation was induced  at low pH  in the presence of varying concentrations 6 
of the surfactant polysorbate 80. Various spectroscopic and imaging  methods were used to study the 7 
aggregation kinetics, as well as structure  and morphology of the formed aggregates . Molecular 8 
dynamics simulations were employed to investigate the initial interaction between the surfactant and 9 
insulin.  Addition of polysorbate 80 slowed down, but did not prevent, agg regation of insulin. Amyloid 10 
spherulites formed under all conditions, with a higher content of intermolecular beta -sheets in the 11 
presence of the surfactant above its critical micelle concentration. In addition, a denser packing was 12 
observed, leading to a m ore stable aggregate. Molecular dynamics simulations suggested a tendency 13 
for insulin to  form dimers in the presence of the surfactant, indicating a change in protein -protein 14 
interactions. It is thus shown that surfactants not only alter aggregation kineti cs, but also affect 15 
physicochemical properties of any aggregates formed . 16

Introduction  1 
Both in vitro  and in vivo  and in destabilizing conditions , proteins  may undergo self -assembly 2 
processes leading to the format ion of a variety of aggregates . A specific class of these aggregates, 3 
named amyloids, are implicated in the onset of human pathologies such as Alzheimer's and 4 
ParkinsonÂ´s disease s (Taylor et al. 2002, Knowles et al. 2014)  and type 2 diabetes (Kahn et al. 1999, 5 
HÃ¶ppener and Lips 2006) . Also,  the impact of protein a ggregates in the development of protein   6 
therapeutics may be significant , resulting in loss of active material  (Wang et al. 2010, van de Weert 7

be a potential risk factor for unwanted immunogenicity  in patients  (Jiskoot et al. 2012) . However, 9

and water purification  (Bolisetty and Mezze nga 2016) . 13

in vivo  proteins may also self-assemble into other  amyloid -like forms, so -called superstructures  16 
(Krebs et al. 2009, Babenko et al. 2011, FoderÃ  and Donald 2014, Vetri and FoderÃ  2015) . Within 17 
this class , amyloid s pherulites are characterized by a fascinating architecture with a spherical 18

et al. 2004, Krebs et al. 2005, Vetri and FoderÃ  2015) . Their radius spans from few  Âµm to mm and 20 
they are readily detectable by cross -polarized light microscopy due to their specific Maltese cross 21

vitro , and especially for globular protein s, spherulites formation can be thermally induced and often 23 . CC-BY-ND 4.0 International license perpetuity. Co-solvents are often employed to tune protein -protein and protein -solvent interactions  (PPIs and 13 
PSIs, respectively)  during the aggregation process  and disentangle  the role of different PPIs in the 14 
reaction  (Vagenende et al. 2009, Bucciarelli et al. 2020) . Among the different co -solvents, surfactants 15 
are common ly used in pharmaceutical science s to tune protein formulation stability . Surfactants 16 
interact with protein s through electrostatic  and hydrophobic interaction s (Otzen 2011)  and reduce 17 
aggregation mainly via suppressing protein adsorption at surfaces (Parkins and Lashmar 2000) ; which 18 
is known to  catalyze the protein refolding reaction (Randolph and Jones 2002) . Moreover,  surfactants 19 
may direct ly bind to hydrophobic patches  on the protein surface, thereby reducing direct PPIs (Jones 20 
1992, Randolph and Jones 2002, Wang et al. 2008)  and lead to an overall enhanced stability of the 21 
protein ensemble (Chou et al. 2005) . Notably, the direct binding between surfactant molecules and 22 
the hydrophobic domains of partially unfolded protein often occurs at the critical micelle 23 . CC-BY-ND 4.0 International license perpetuity. While the enhanced protein stability induced by surfactants is well documented , very limited 3 
information is available on the effect of surfactants on protein aggregation processes and on the 4 
features of the  final aggregate  structures , the latter being of vital importance for risk assessment of 5 
therapeutics . Indeed, secondary structure content, size , morphology and geometrical packing may 6 
alter the capability of the aggrega tes to induce immunoresponse (Filipe et al. 2010) . Siposova  et al. 7 
(2019 ) reported that the non -ionic surfactant Triton â„¢ X-100 affected insulin amyloid aggregation  at 8 
acidic pH  depend ing on the protein :surfactant  ratio, demonstrating that at concentration s below CMC  9 
insulin fibrillation was partially inhibited  and fibril morphology was greatly altered . Inhibition of in 10 
vitro  fibril formation was observed on a time scale of approximately 2 hours  when the concentration 11 
of detergent was above the CMC  (Siposova et al. 2019) ; this being ascribed to different  binding 12 
phenomena between Triton â„¢ X-100 and insulin dimer s.  13 
In the present work, we aim at investigat ing the mechanisms by which  polysorbate 80 (PS80 ) 14 
affects the stability and aggregation propensity of human insulin , with a special  focus on  the 15 
formation of aggregates other than fibrils, i.e. amyloid spherulites , and the related structural and 16 
morphological features . PS80  is a non -ionic surfactant and one of the main excipients added in protein 17 
formulations to maintain protein therapeutic activity and prolong the shelf life of the formulations  18 
(Jones et al. 2018) . Using a combination of spectroscopy and imaging methods , we reveal that PS80  19 
does not inhibit  the thermal aggregation  process for insulin  on a time scale of approximately 2 days , 20 
but rather delays the process . Surprisingly , addition of PS80  leads to  an increase in Î²-sheet structure 21 
and concomitant altered morphology and enhanced stability of the formed spherulites . This 22 
demonstrates  the importance of not only investigating the impact of excipient concentration on 23 . CC-BY-ND 4.0 International license perpetuity.  3.5 PS80  Affects the Stability of Insulin Spherulites in Alkaline Conditions  7 
The previous sections show that the addition of PS80  changed the aggregation kinetics, morphology, 8 
and molecular structure of insulin spherulites, which may relate to differences in aggre gate stability. 9 
Electrostatics forces are known to affect the stability of amyloid aggregates, and alkaline conditions 10 
promote the disassembly of insulin amyloid fibrils, leading to a release of soluble insulin molecules  11 
(Shammas et al. 2011, Santangelo et al. 2016) . The stability of spherulites were therefore tested 12 
against critical pH change by dispersing them in an a queous solution at pH 9 to achieve disassembly 13 
kinetics within a reasonable time frame .  14 
Figure 6a displays the dissociation kinetics of insulin spherulites formed at different PS80  15 
concentrations. Specifically, we monitored the concentration of soluble insulin released from the 16 
aggregates over time in boric acid buffer at pH 9 relative to the total insulin content in the spherulites.  17

Conclusions  5 
In this  study, w e present the effect of PS80  on the self -assembly mechanism, structure, morphology 6 
and stability of insulin amyloid -like spherulites. PS80  induce d a delay in the onset  of amyloid 7 
spherulites formation . This delay may be ascribed to the  ability of PS80  to induce  insulin dimers with 8 
increased stability against aggregation  and to modify the surface properties of the sample holder, 9 
reducing surface -catalysed aggregation . Interestingly, t he addition of this surfactant did not affect  the 10 
total amount of insulin converted into aggregates. However, at increasing PS80  concentration, the 11 
level of clustering of amyloid spherulites  was greatly affected,  with a threshold around the CMC 12 
value for the surfactant . Moreover, w hen formed in solution with [ PS80 ] > CMC, the spherulites 13 
acquire d a very dense packing of the structure with an increase in  the amount of  Î²-sheet structure. 14 
This confer red an increased stability to the spherulites  when exposed to  alkaline conditions . Avoiding 15 
the appearance of protein particles  in pharmaceutical products is one of the most challenging tasks in 16 
pharmaceutical science s. The presence of surfactants like polysorbates is known to delay protein 17 
aggregation. Our findings point out that the ad dition of PS80  may alter not only the protein 18 
aggregation kinetics, but also the physicochemical properties of the  protein  aggregates.  Such a change 19 
in properties is not obvious from single morphological assessment s and requires a set of  specific 20 
analytical methods. Further investigation is needed to evaluate w hether the change in aggregate 21 
properties highlighted in our work has any impact on safety and efficacy. Importantly , using 22
